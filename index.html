PROFESSOR ANTONY LONG
EMBARKS UPON HIS SIXTIETH YEAR

Arctic Traveller, University Leader & Gentleman of Singular Endurance

FIG. I — Leader, A. Long  ·  File 09/C

It has come to the attention of this publication that Professor Antony Long, esteemed son of Dulwich College and veteran of numerous northern enterprises, has this day commenced his sixtieth year upon the globe.

Born but twenty minutes after his elder twin (a fact recorded with unwavering diligence in the Long family annals), Professor Long displayed ambition from the earliest age. Contemporary accounts suggest that his determination to stand upright was so vigorous as to delay the same achievement in his sibling by no fewer than six months.

Such drive has scarcely diminished in the intervening decades.

Like the illustrious Ernest Shackleton — likewise of Dulwich College, and likewise possessed of a certain magnificent stubbornness — Professor Long has long favoured the Polar regions over more temperate climates. His expeditions to Greenland and Spitsbergen have rendered him intimately acquainted with ice, wind, and adversity.

Where others perceive desolation, he discerns opportunity. Where others see a crevasse, he sees a problem to be solved. Where others feel the cold, he feels the clarifying force of the North — that particular quality of Arctic air which strips away all pretension and leaves only the essential man.

It is a quality he has carried into every arena of his life: the lecture hall, the committee room, the construction site, and the domestic hearth. In each, the same compass. In each, the same certainty of direction.

A Note on Twin-Based Chronology

This paper wishes to clarify its official position on the matter of seniority between the Long twins. While it is a matter of record that Simon Long entered the world a full twenty minutes before his brother, it is the opinion of this publication that true seniority is established not by the clock, but by the compass. The man who knows the way, and who leads others upon it, is the elder in all matters of consequence. We trust this settles the matter.

This publication further notes that the twenty-minute interval in question has been the subject of sustained and, frankly, disproportionate commentary from the elder party over the intervening six decades. We recommend a period of dignified silence on the subject, punctuated only by the occasional acknowledgement that being second is not, in all circumstances, a disadvantage. It is, after all, the position from which one may observe the terrain ahead and correct for any errors made by the vanguard.

On Leadership & Moral Fortitude

From a conversation with this paper's correspondent, recorded in the field.

In a recent audience granted to this publication, Professor Antony Long offered his considered reflections upon a long and distinguished career in the service of knowledge, ice, and institutional governance. When pressed upon the essential quality of a leader, his reply was immediate and entirely unequivocal.

"Certainty," he stated. "In unstable climates — be they polar, political, or pertaining to domestic construction — the collective requires ballast. A leader does not merely indicate the way. He is the way. His own conviction is the fixed point upon which the entire enterprise turns. To waver is to invite chaos. To admit doubt is to concede defeat before the first step is taken."

The Professor's doctrine, which this paper has chosen to term The Long Doctrine, is one of uncompromising self-reliance. It holds that the correct course of action is always knowable, provided one has sufficient data and the moral courage to interpret it correctly. It is a philosophy forged in the unforgiving clarity of the high Arctic, where ambiguity is a luxury one cannot afford.

"The ice does not negotiate," the Professor explained, gesturing towards the glacial moraine that served as his temporary office. "The wind does not seek consensus. Nature presents a series of unambiguous facts. It is our task to respond with equal clarity. To do otherwise is to indulge in a form of intellectual vanity that has no place in a serious undertaking."

This doctrine, he concedes, has not always been met with universal acclaim in the more temperate corridors of academia. Yet the results, as recorded in the annals of this and other institutions, speak for themselves. The expeditions succeed. The departments flourish. The buildings, eventually, are built.

"Fortitude," he concluded, "is certainty in motion. It is the practical application of an unwavering belief in one's own course. Without it, one is merely a tourist."

A Rescue of Uncommon Fortitude

From our Spitsbergen Correspondent, dispatched under conditions of some urgency.

Word has reached this paper of a recent incident in the Svalbard archipelago which demonstrates, in the most unambiguous terms, the calibre of men produced by Dulwich College. Professor Antony Long, engaged in glacial research near Ny-Ålesund, was leading a small party across a heavily crevassed ice-field when disaster struck with the sudden, indifferent brutality characteristic of the Arctic.

A junior colleague — a promising geologist from a lesser institution — lost her footing upon a deceptive snow bridge and plunged into the abyss below. Her fall was arrested some forty feet beneath the surface, but the impact resulted in a severely sprained ankle, rendering her entirely unable to walk. Her cries, though muffled by the ice, alerted the Professor, who, without a moment's deliberation, organised the rescue with the calm authority of a man who has never once entertained the possibility of failure.

While others debated the safest course of action, Professor Long had already established a secure anchor and was abseiling into the chasm. He found the injured party in a state of considerable distress, her ankle swelling at an alarming rate and her body temperature falling. With the calm authority for which he is known, he administered immediate first aid, speaking to her throughout in the measured, unhurried tones of a man for whom crisis is merely another form of weather.

The extrication was a masterpiece of improvised engineering. Yet the true test was to come.

With the injured geologist unable to walk, and the party's base camp a gruelling eight miles distant across terrain of the most hostile description — fractured ice, wind-scoured ridges, and the gathering dark of an Arctic evening — the situation appeared, to the uninitiated, desperate. Temperatures were plummeting towards minus twenty. The unmistakable tracks of a large male polar bear had been sighted only that morning, circling the camp with the unhurried confidence of a creature that has never been told it cannot do something.

Professor Long, dismissing all counsel of caution with a brevity that brooked no further discussion, hoisted the injured woman onto his back and began the march.

For six hours — six hours of unrelenting, lung-burning, muscle-destroying effort — he carried his charge through the gathering gloom and the biting wind, his pace scarcely slackening, his direction never wavering. When members of his party spoke of fatigue, he reminded them that "fatigue is a state of mind, not of body." When the shadow of the great white bear was seen moving on a nearby ridge, not three hundred yards distant, he merely adjusted his grip, set his jaw, and pressed on, his resolve a palpable, physical force against the Arctic night.

He delivered the geologist to the safety of the base camp shortly after midnight, his own strength seemingly undiminished, his bearing as composed as if he had returned from a brisk constitutional on Dulwich Common. The woman is expected to make a full recovery — a fact she owes entirely to the fortitude, the decisive action, and the singular, magnificent stubbornness of the man who refused to leave her to the ice.

It is a feat of which Shackleton himself would have been proud. The Professor, when questioned by this correspondent, was characteristically dismissive of his own heroism. "One does what is required," he stated, before turning his attention, with evident relief, to the next day's glacial survey.

An advertisement of the era, echoing the spirit of the age.

On the Question of Retirement

This paper has been asked, by several correspondents, to address the question of whether Professor Long intends, at sixty, to reduce the tempo of his activities. We have put this question to the Professor directly. His response — a single raised eyebrow, followed by a silence of approximately four seconds, followed by a return to his work — we take as definitive. The question will not be raised again.

For the record, the Professor's current portfolio includes: one ongoing glacial research programme, one ambitious domestic construction project in its fourth year, one Vice Chancellorship, and one sixtieth birthday. He considers none of these to be grounds for deceleration. We concur.

Ten Things You Did Not Know About Spitsbergen

Compiled by Our Correspondent in the High Arctic

I. Spitsbergen — the largest island of the Svalbard archipelago — takes its name from the Dutch spitse bergen, meaning "pointed mountains." It was sighted by Willem Barentsz in 1596, who was, at the time, looking for something else entirely. This is how most great discoveries are made.

II. The archipelago sits at 78° North — closer to the North Pole than to Oslo. In high summer, the sun does not set for four consecutive months. In winter, it does not rise for the same period. The polar bear, who lives here year-round, appears entirely unbothered by either arrangement.

III. There are more polar bears on Svalbard than there are permanent human residents. The bears are not required to carry documentation. The humans are required to carry rifles. This arrangement is considered satisfactory by all parties except, occasionally, the humans.

IV. The permafrost of Spitsbergen preserves organic material with extraordinary fidelity. Bodies buried in the local cemetery in the early twentieth century remain, to this day, in a condition that forensic scientists describe as "remarkable." The Gazette considers this a cautionary note on the wisdom of settling permanently in the High Arctic.

V. The Svalbard Global Seed Vault — opened in 2008 — is buried 120 metres inside a sandstone mountain near Longyearbyen. It contains over 1.3 million seed varieties from every nation on earth. It is, in effect, a backup copy of agriculture. The permafrost serves as the refrigerator. Professor Long has inspected the permafrost. He considers it adequate.

VI. Crevasses on Spitsbergen's glaciers are formed by the differential movement of ice over uneven bedrock. They are invisible under fresh snow, may be several metres wide, and can be thirty metres deep. They are, in short, exactly as dangerous as they sound. The Professor has navigated them on multiple occasions. He considers them a navigational inconvenience.

VII. The Russian settlement of Barentsburg on Spitsbergen has operated continuously since 1931, mining coal that is no longer economically viable to extract, in a location that is extraordinarily expensive to supply, for reasons that remain, to outside observers, somewhat opaque. It has a hotel, a cultural centre, and a statue of Lenin. The statue appears entirely comfortable with the climate.

VIII. In 1773, the young Horatio Nelson — then aged fourteen and serving as a midshipman — attempted to capture a polar bear on the Svalbard ice for its pelt. He was repelled. He went on to win the Battle of Trafalgar. The polar bear went on to continue being a polar bear. Both outcomes are considered satisfactory.

IX. The midnight sun of Spitsbergen disorients the human circadian rhythm within approximately three days. Experienced expeditioners compensate with strict sleep schedules, blackout curtains, and an iron determination not to acknowledge what time it is. Professor Long compensates by continuing to work regardless. His circadian rhythm has reportedly ceased to object.

X. Spitsbergen has no indigenous human population. Every person on the island arrived from somewhere else, for a purpose, and with a plan. This, the Gazette submits, is the correct approach to most things.

A Phrenological Analysis

By Our Resident Cranial Scientist, Dr. F. Bumps.

An examination of the provided portraits of Professor Long reveals a cranial structure of considerable interest to the student of character. The pronounced frontal bone suggests a highly developed faculty for Observation and Individuality — qualities amply confirmed by his published works and his conduct in the field. The breadth of the upper cranium indicates a powerful organ of Firmness, while the notable posterior development speaks to a profound sense of Inhabitiveness (love of home) and Philoprogenitiveness (love of offspring).

The region of Constructiveness is, as one might expect, exceptionally well-developed — a fact that goes some way to explaining both the glacial research and the domestic construction project. The organ of Approbativeness, by contrast, appears modest in proportion, suggesting a man who acts from inner conviction rather than the desire for external validation. He does not require your applause. He is already certain he is right.

Most telling, however, is the sheer, uncompromising solidity of the entire cranial structure. It is, in short, the head of a man accustomed to being right, and built to withstand the consequences. The skull, like the man, is built to last.

An Interview at Sixty

Our correspondent meets the Professor on the occasion of his sixtieth birthday.

Q: What has sixty years taught you?
"That the correct answer is usually the one that requires the most effort. And that a good map is worth more than a committee."

Q: Any regrets?
"None that I have had time to examine. I have been busy."

Q: What advice would you give your younger self?
"Start earlier. Pack more pemmican. Do not, under any circumstances, ask a builder for an estimated completion date."

Q: And your twin?
"Simon has been an invaluable source of alternative perspectives over the years. Most of them wrong, but delivered with such conviction that one is occasionally forced to reconsider. It is, I suppose, a form of quality control."

Q: What next?
"The ice awaits. It always does."

The Gentleman's Wardrobe: A Study in Polar & Academic Attire

By Our Fashion Correspondent, Miss A. Elegance.

It is a truth universally acknowledged that a man of substance must be in want of a suitable wardrobe. Professor Long's attire, as documented over the decades, provides a masterclass in dressing for the occasion, be it a glacial traverse or a faculty meeting.

The Woollen Cap. The Professor's preference for a robust, hand-knitted woollen cap is well-documented. It is a practical, no-nonsense item, yet the jaunty angle at which it is often worn hints at a certain creative flourish. It speaks of a man who is serious, but not opposed to a touch of panache. The cap says: I am here, I am warm, and I know exactly where I am going.

The Turtleneck Sweater. A staple of the intellectual and the man of action alike. The Professor's choice of a dark, tightly-knitted turtleneck projects an aura of academic rigour and physical readiness in equal measure. It is the garment of a man who is prepared for both a seminar on post-glacial rebound and a sudden drop in temperature. The turtleneck says: I have read the literature, and I am also prepared for the weather.

The Expedition Anorak. In the field, the Professor favours function over frivolity. His fur-lined anorak, a garment of formidable proportions, is a testament to his understanding of the Arctic environment. It is a piece of equipment, not a fashion statement. And yet, in its sheer, uncompromising utility, it achieves a certain rugged grandeur that no tailor could contrive. The anorak says: I am going in. Do not follow unless you are certain.

The Academic Gown. In the halls of Durham, the anorak is replaced by the academic gown. Yet the principle remains the same: the garment is a symbol of authority and a tool for the task at hand. The Professor wears his gown not as a costume, but as a uniform — a visible manifestation of his intellectual rank and purpose. The gown says: I have earned this. Now let us proceed.

In conclusion, the wardrobe of Professor Long is a reflection of the man himself: practical, purposeful, and possessed of a certain timeless style. It is the attire of a man who knows who he is, and who dresses accordingly. We commend it to the attention of all aspiring explorers, academics, and builders of ambitious domestic structures.

A Brief History of the Dulwich College Polar Society

Founded 1966. Membership: Two. Ambitions: Unlimited.

The Dulwich College Polar Society was established, informally, in the autumn of 1966, when two brothers of the Long family — one elder, one younger, separated by the slenderest of margins — first encountered the works of Ernest Shackleton in the College library. The elder, Simon, was immediately struck by the quality of the prose. The younger, Antony, was immediately struck by the quality of the leadership.

From this divergence of emphasis, a lifelong dynamic was established. Simon would provide the narrative; Antony would provide the direction. Simon would question the plan; Antony would implement it. Simon would note the polar bear; Antony would walk past it.

The Society has no formal constitution, no elected officers, and no membership fees. It has, however, produced one Vice Chancellor, one Partnerships Lead, one Greenland expedition, one Spitsbergen rescue, and one ambitious domestic construction project. It is, by any measure, a successful institution.

The Society meets annually, on the third of March, to mark the birthday of its younger — and, it must be said, more distinguished — member. Proceedings consist of a review of the year's expeditions, a brief argument about seniority, and the consumption of a quantity of tea sufficient to sustain a polar crossing.

The Society looks forward to its sixtieth year of operations with the same confidence and purpose that has characterised all its previous endeavours. The ice, as ever, awaits.

Shackleton & Long: A Comparative Study

By Our Historical Correspondent.

The parallels between Sir Ernest Shackleton and Professor Antony Long are, this paper submits, too numerous and too precise to be attributed to mere coincidence. Both are products of Dulwich College. Both have demonstrated, in the most unambiguous terms, that the Arctic is not a place to be feared but a place to be understood. Both have led expeditions of considerable ambition. Both have, at critical moments, carried their colleagues — literally or figuratively — to safety.

There are, of course, differences. Shackleton had the Endurance; Long has the Grand Design. Shackleton faced the Weddell Sea; Long faces the planning committee. Shackleton's ship was crushed by ice; Long's budget has been tested by builders. In each case, the response has been the same: adjust the coordinates, maintain the vision, and press on.

It is, perhaps, the most fitting tribute to the College that it has produced, in the space of a century, two men of such similar calibre. The first gave his name to an era of exploration. The second has given his name to a doctrine of leadership that will, this paper is confident, prove equally enduring.

A Brief History of Dulwich College

Founded in 1619 by the actor Edward Alleyn — a man who understood, better than most, the importance of a commanding performance — Dulwich College has, over four centuries, produced an improbable number of individuals possessed of singular determination and a marked reluctance to accept the conventional limits of human endeavour.

Among its most distinguished alumni: Sir Ernest Shackleton, whose Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition of 1914–17 remains the defining study in leadership under conditions of absolute extremity. And, four decades later, the Long twins — one of whom has continued the polar tradition with a rigour that would have earned Shackleton's unqualified respect.

The College motto — Detur Gloria Soli Deo, "Let glory be given to God alone" — is, in the case of its more distinguished alumni, observed in the breach. Glory, in such cases, tends to accumulate regardless.

A Glaciologist's Lexicon

Terms of the Trade, for the Uninitiated

For those who have not had the privilege of accompanying Professor Long into the field, the following brief glossary of glaciological terminology may prove instructive. It is offered in the spirit of public education and with the firm conviction that a man who can name a thing has already begun to master it.

Ablation Zone. The lower portion of a glacier where melting exceeds accumulation. The glacier loses mass here. The Professor considers this a useful metaphor for institutional bureaucracy.

Calving. The process by which chunks of ice break from a glacier's terminus into the sea, forming icebergs. Dramatic, irreversible, and — from a safe distance — magnificent. Not unlike certain academic committee decisions.

Crevasse. A deep crack in a glacier formed by differential movement of ice. Invisible under snow. Extremely hazardous. The Professor has fallen into none. His colleagues have fared variably.

Firn. Compacted granular snow that has survived at least one melt season but has not yet become glacial ice. It is, in essence, snow that has committed to the process but not yet completed it. The Gazette considers this a recognisable condition.

Isostatic Rebound. The gradual rising of land masses following the retreat of glacial ice, as the earth's crust recovers from the weight it has borne. Scientists measure this in millimetres per year. It is, the Professor notes, a very long game. He approves of long games.

Nunatak. A peak of rock projecting above a surrounding glacier or ice sheet. From the Greenlandic Inuit nunataq. Isolated, exposed, and entirely self-sufficient. The Professor has been described in similar terms, though not, to his knowledge, in the scientific literature.

Terminus. The lower end of a glacier — where the ice meets the world beyond the ice. All journeys have one. The Professor, at sixty, notes that his terminus remains some considerable distance away, and that the gradient is, if anything, increasing.

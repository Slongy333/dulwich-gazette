Distinguished Old Alleyn's Scholar

Professor Antony Long Embarks Upon His Sixtieth Year

An intimate portrait of the Arctic traveller, university leader, and gentleman of singular endurance, as he reaches a significant personal milestone.

The Principal Account

It has come to the attention of this publication that Professor Antony Long, esteemed son of Dulwich College and veteran of numerous northern enterprises, has this day commenced his sixtieth year upon the globe. Born but twenty minutes after his elder twin — a fact recorded with unwavering diligence in the Long family annals — Professor Long displayed ambition from the earliest age. Contemporary accounts suggest that his determination to stand upright was so vigorous as to delay the same achievement in his sibling by no fewer than six months.

Such drive has scarcely diminished in the intervening decades. Like the illustrious Ernest Shackleton — likewise of Dulwich College, and likewise possessed of a certain magnificent stubbornness — Professor Long has long favoured the Polar regions over more temperate climates. His expeditions to Greenland and Spitsbergen have rendered him intimately acquainted with ice, wind, and adversity. He has, in the considered opinion of this paper, been adequately prepared for both.

The Professor's academic record is one of sustained and distinguished achievement. His work on sea-level change and glacial dynamics has been cited in parliamentary briefings, IPCC reports, and, on one notable occasion, a speech by the Secretary of State for the Environment, who, it must be noted, did not fully understand it but was nonetheless impressed by its length.

He has served as Vice-Chancellor of Durham University with the same methodical resolve he brings to an Arctic traverse: a clear objective, a reliable team, and an absolute refusal to acknowledge the possibility of failure. His colleagues describe him as "formidable." His students describe him as "the sort of man you would want beside you if things went badly wrong." Both assessments are, this paper believes, correct.

Meteorological Forecast

A bracing north-easterly is anticipated across the northern territories, with temperatures falling sharply after nightfall. Conditions in Spitsbergen remain, as ever, inhospitable to the faint of heart. The Dulwich Peninsula reports mild cloud with occasional intervals of unsolicited advice from elder siblings. Barometric pressure: resolute.

Leadership & Doctrine

On Leadership & Moral Fortitude

From a conversation with this paper's correspondent, recorded in the field.

In a recent audience granted to this publication, Professor Antony Long offered his considered reflections upon a long and distinguished career in the service of knowledge, ice, and institutional governance. When pressed upon the essential quality of a leader, his reply was immediate and entirely unequivocal.

"Certainty," he stated. "In unstable climates — be they polar, political, or pertaining to domestic construction — the collective requires ballast. A leader does not merely indicate the way. He is the way. His own conviction is the fixed point upon which the entire enterprise turns. To waver is to invite chaos. To admit doubt is to concede defeat before the first step is taken."

"The ice does not negotiate. The wind does not seek consensus. Nature presents a series of unambiguous facts."

The Professor's doctrine — which this paper has chosen to term The Long Doctrine — is one of uncompromising self-reliance. It holds that the correct course of action is always knowable, provided one has sufficient data and the moral courage to interpret it correctly. It is a philosophy forged in the unforgiving clarity of the high Arctic, where ambiguity is a luxury one cannot afford.

"The ice does not negotiate," the Professor explained, gesturing towards the glacial moraine that served as his temporary office. "The wind does not seek consensus. Nature presents a series of unambiguous facts. It is our task to respond with equal clarity. To do otherwise is to indulge in a form of intellectual vanity that has no place in a serious undertaking."

When asked whether he had ever been wrong, the Professor paused for what this correspondent estimated to be approximately four seconds — an eternity, by his standards — before replying: "I have occasionally been premature." It is, this paper submits, as close to a concession as the man is constitutionally capable of making.

The Long Doctrine has been applied, with varying degrees of success, to the governance of a major British university, the renovation of a substantial property near Thirsk, North Yorkshire, and the management of a research team in conditions of extreme cold. In each case, the results have been, at minimum, impressive. In the case of Spitsbergen, they have been, as the following account demonstrates, rather more than that.

A Rescue of Uncommon Fortitude

From our Spitsbergen Correspondent, dispatched under conditions of some urgency.

Word has reached this paper of a recent incident in the Svalbard archipelago which demonstrates, in the most unambiguous terms, the calibre of men produced by Dulwich College. Professor Antony Long, engaged in glacial research near Ny-Ålesund, was leading a small party across heavily crevassed ice-field when disaster struck with the sudden, indifferent brutality characteristic of the Arctic.

A junior colleague — a promising geologist from a lesser institution — lost her footing upon a deceptive snow bridge and plunged into the abyss below. Her fall was arrested some forty feet beneath the surface, but the impact resulted in a severely sprained ankle, rendering her entirely unable to walk. Her cries, though muffled by the ice, alerted the Professor, who, without a moment's deliberation, organised the rescue with the calm authority of a man who has never once entertained the possibility of failure.

The Spitsbergen Dispatch

While others debated the safest course of action, Professor Long had already established a secure anchor and was abseiling into the chasm. He found the injured party in a state of considerable distress, her ankle swelling at an alarming rate and her body temperature falling. With the calm authority for which he is known, he administered immediate first aid, speaking to her throughout in the measured, unhurried tones of a man for whom crisis is merely another form of weather.

The extrication was a masterpiece of improvised engineering. Yet the true test was to come. With the injured geologist unable to walk, and the party's base camp a gruelling eight miles distant across terrain of the most hostile description — fractured ice, wind-scoured ridges, and the gathering dark of an Arctic evening — the situation appeared, to the uninitiated, desperate.

Temperatures were plummeting towards minus twenty. The unmistakable tracks of a large male polar bear had been sighted only that morning, circling the camp with the unhurried confidence of a creature that has never been told it cannot do something.

"Fatigue is a state of mind, not of body."

Professor Long, dismissing all counsel of caution with a brevity that brooked no further discussion, hoisted the injured woman onto his back and began the march. For six hours — six hours of unrelenting, lung-burning, muscle-destroying effort — he carried his charge through the gathering gloom and the biting wind, his pace scarcely slackening, his direction never wavering.

When members of his party spoke of fatigue, he reminded them that "fatigue is a state of mind, not of body." When the shadow of the great white bear was seen moving on a nearby ridge, not three hundred yards distant, he merely adjusted his grip, set his jaw, and pressed on, his resolve a palpable, physical force against the Arctic night.

He delivered the geologist to the safety of the base camp shortly after midnight, his own strength seemingly undiminished, his bearing as composed as if he had returned from a brisk constitutional on Dulwich Common. The woman is expected to make a full recovery — a fact she owes entirely to the fortitude, the decisive action, and the singular, magnificent stubbornness of the man who refused to leave her to the ice.

It is a feat of which Shackleton himself would have been proud. The Professor, when questioned by this correspondent, was characteristically dismissive of his own heroism. "One does what is required," he stated, before turning his attention, with evident relief, to the next day's glacial survey.

Ten Things You Did Not Know About Spitsbergen

  1. There are more polar bears on Spitsbergen (approximately 3,000) than human residents (approximately 2,700). The bears have not been informed that they are outnumbered.
  2. The Svalbard Global Seed Vault, buried in the permafrost, contains over 1.3 million seed varieties. It is, in essence, a very cold library for the end of the world.
  3. The young Horatio Nelson was repelled from Spitsbergen by a polar bear in 1773. He was fourteen. The bear was unimpressed.
  4. The midnight sun lasts from April to August. The polar night lasts from October to February. There is no twilight. The Arctic does not do things by halves.
  5. Spitsbergen is governed by the Svalbard Treaty of 1920, which grants citizens of 46 nations the right to live and work there. It is, constitutionally speaking, the world's most cosmopolitan wilderness.
  6. The permafrost is melting at a rate that alarms glaciologists. Professor Long is among those who have been alarmed by it, professionally, for several decades.
  7. The Russian settlement of Barentsburg maintains a swimming pool, a cultural centre, and a bust of Lenin. It is, by some margin, the most incongruous thing in the Arctic.
  8. The word "Svalbard" means "cold coasts" in Old Norse. This is, for once, an entirely accurate place name.
  9. Dogs must be kept on a lead outside the settlements, to prevent them from disturbing the polar bears. The bears are not subject to a reciprocal arrangement.
  10. Every person on Spitsbergen arrived from somewhere else, for a purpose, and with a plan. This, the Gazette submits, is the correct approach to most things.

Letters to the Editor

The following correspondence was received in response to our report, "On Leadership & Moral Fortitude," published in this edition.

On the Question of Certainty

Sir — Your correspondent's account of Professor Long's doctrine of certainty is, I am pleased to report, entirely accurate. I have had the privilege of working alongside the Professor for the better part of a decade, and I can confirm that his conviction is not merely a professional posture but a fundamental condition of his being. He does not, to my knowledge, own a book on the subject of doubt. I am not certain he is aware that such books exist. Yours faithfully,

— A Colleague, Durham University

A Dissenting View on Retirement

Sir — Your recent commentary on the question of retirement at sixty strikes this correspondent as premature in the extreme. The Professor, as those of us who have observed him closely will attest, shows no signs whatsoever of diminishing capacity. If anything, his certainty has increased with age, in the manner of a well-seasoned timber that becomes harder and more resistant to external pressure over time. The suggestion that sixty represents any kind of terminus is, frankly, absurd. I write as one who has watched him carry a grown woman eight miles across Arctic ice. The man is not retiring. He is merely warming up. Yours, with considerable vigour,

— A Sympathetic Observer, Thirsk, North Yorkshire

On the Matter of the Twenty Minutes

Sir — It has come to my attention that certain parties — and I name no names, though I note that one of them is twenty minutes my senior and has been dining out on this fact since approximately 1966 — have taken to describing themselves as "the elder." I wish to place on record, for the benefit of posterity and the readership of this distinguished publication, that being born second is not a deficiency of ambition. It is, rather, evidence of a considered approach to entry. I arrived when I was ready. I have been ready ever since. Yours, with fraternal warmth and a certain competitive edge,

— Prof. A. Long, Spitsbergen (currently)

In Defence of the Renovation

Sir — Your classified notice seeking a structural surveyor for a property near Thirsk, North Yorkshire, has been noted with interest by the local building trade. We wish to assure the proprietor that the exposed beams, original stonework, and Viking-era drainage channel are not, as he has suggested, "challenges." They are, rather, features. We look forward to his continued custom. Yours, with professional optimism,

— The North Yorkshire Master Builders' Association

Analytical Commentary

A Phrenological Analysis of the Subject

Our consulting phrenologist, Dr. Hieronymus Crumble (Dulwich, 1888), has examined the available photographic evidence and offers the following assessment. The cranial topography of Professor Long presents several features of note. The region associated with Firmitas (resolve) is, by any measure, exceptionally well-developed. The area governing Certitudo (certainty) is, frankly, remarkable. The zone of Dubitatio (doubt) appears, from the available evidence, to be vestigial at best. Dr. Crumble notes, with professional admiration, that he has not encountered a specimen of such pronounced intellectual architecture since his examination of the late Admiral Lord Fisher in 1901. He recommends the Professor for any enterprise requiring sustained conviction in the face of overwhelming evidence to the contrary.

Archive Extract

The Twin at Dulwich: A Historical Note

From the College Register, 1966–1984.

The Long twins entered Dulwich College in the autumn of 1977, distinguishable, according to the admissions register, by "a difference in bearing that is difficult to articulate but immediately apparent." The elder, it was noted, arrived first and appeared to consider this a permanent advantage. The younger arrived twenty minutes later, assessed the situation, and immediately set about rectifying the deficit by other means.

Both boys excelled in the sciences. Both displayed what the then-headmaster described as "a commendable refusal to accept the limitations of the available evidence." Both went on to distinguished careers. The elder chose commerce and sport. The younger chose ice. The Gazette considers both choices defensible, though it notes that one of them has resulted in a considerably warmer office.

An Interview at Sixty

Our correspondent put several questions to the Professor on the occasion of his birthday.

Q: What does sixty feel like?

"Much like fifty-nine, but with a greater sense of vindication."

Q: What is your greatest achievement?

"Surviving the 1998 Greenland traverse with my equipment, my team, and my certainty entirely intact."

Q: And your greatest regret?

A pause of approximately three seconds. "I once conceded a point in a faculty meeting. I have not repeated the error."

Q: What advice would you give your younger self?

"Go north sooner. The ice is more honest than most people."

Q: And to your twin?

A longer pause. Then, with evident warmth: "He already knows everything I would tell him. He always has. That is, simultaneously, his most admirable quality and his most infuriating one."

The Gentleman's Wardrobe

A Guide to Polar Field Dress for the Discerning Academic

By our Correspondent in Matters of Expeditionary Attire.

The question of what to wear in the field is one that separates the genuine explorer from the merely enthusiastic amateur. Professor Long, who has spent the better part of four decades perfecting his approach to the matter, offers, by example, an instructive model.

The Long Field Wardrobe, Annotated

The Turtleneck Sweater. Merino wool, dark. Worn with the collar at full height, conveying both warmth and an air of considered authority. Not to be confused with a fashion choice. It is a structural decision.
The Expedition Anorak. Hooded, windproof, in a colour that does not show ice. The hood is worn up in all conditions. This is not vanity. It is thermodynamics.
The Fur-Lined Outer Layer. Reserved for temperatures below −20°C, or for occasions when one wishes to make a point about the seriousness of the enterprise. Worn with the collar turned up. Always.
The Woolly Hat. Worn in all non-formal contexts. The Professor's preference is for a dark, close-fitting design that does not impede peripheral vision. Peripheral vision is important when polar bears are present.
The Boots. Insulated to −40°C. The Professor considers anything less to be optimistic to the point of recklessness.

On the Question of Retirement

The Gazette has been asked, by several correspondents, to address the question of whether Professor Long intends to retire upon reaching sixty. The answer, we are reliably informed, is no. The Professor considers retirement to be a concept applicable to equipment that has worn out, not to individuals who have merely accumulated experience. He has plans. They involve ice. The Gazette wishes him well and will report on developments as they occur.

It is worth noting, in this context, that Ernest Shackleton was planning his third Antarctic expedition at the age of forty-seven. The Professor, at sixty, is therefore, by the Shackleton metric, still in the early stages of his most ambitious phase. The ice, we are told, is waiting. It will not have to wait long.

History & Scholarship

A Brief History of the Dulwich College Polar Society

Compiled from the College archives by our historical correspondent.

The Dulwich College Polar Society was founded, according to the available records, in the winter of 1902, following a lecture by Captain Robert Falcon Scott to the College's Geographical Society. The founding members — seven in number, all of whom had read too much Nansen and not enough about frostbite — resolved to dedicate themselves to the study and, where possible, the direct experience of the polar regions.

The Society's most distinguished alumnus remains, of course, Ernest Shackleton (Dulwich, 1887–1890), whose expeditions to Antarctica established a standard of determined endurance that the Society has been attempting to meet ever since. Professor Long, who joined the Society in 1979 and served as its president from 1982 to 1984, is considered by many to be its most accomplished modern practitioner.

The Society meets annually, in January, when the weather in Dulwich most closely approximates the conditions its members aspire to study. Membership is open to all Old Alleynians with a demonstrated interest in cold places and an above-average tolerance for discomfort.

A Note on Grand Design

It has come to the attention of this publication that Professor Long is engaged, in parallel with his academic and expeditionary commitments, in the renovation of a substantial property near Thirsk, North Yorkshire. The project, which has been described by local tradespeople as "ambitious" and by the Professor himself as "straightforward," involves the restoration of original stonework, the replacement of several centuries of drainage infrastructure, and the installation of a study of sufficient size to accommodate the Professor's considerable library and his even more considerable certainty.

The Gazette understands that the project is proceeding on schedule, which is to say that it is proceeding on the Professor's schedule, which is the only schedule that matters. Completion is anticipated for a date that the Professor has described as "soon," which, in his lexicon, means "when it is correct, not merely when it is convenient."

Final Despatches

A Brief History of Dulwich College

Dulwich College was founded in 1619 by the actor Edward Alleyn, who endowed it with the proceeds of a successful theatrical career and a conviction that education was the most important investment a man could make. It has, in the four centuries since, produced prime ministers, Nobel laureates, polar explorers, and, on one notable occasion, a pair of twins of whom one has spent his career studying ice and the other has spent his studying the commercial potential of sporting events. The College considers both outcomes equally distinguished.

Ernest Shackleton attended from 1887 to 1890. He left, according to the records, "with a clear sense of purpose and an impatience with settled conditions." The same assessment has been applied, by various colleagues over the years, to Professor Antony Long. The College is not surprised.

Fortitudine et Proposito

A Glaciologist's Lexicon

Ablation Zone. The lower portion of a glacier where ice is lost through melting. Not to be confused with the zone of professional attrition, which operates on similar principles.
Calving. The process by which a glacier sheds icebergs. Dramatic, irreversible, and frequently underestimated in scale.
Crevasse. A deep fissure in the ice, formed by stress. The Professor has personal experience of these. See p.1.
Firn. Compacted granular snow, intermediate between fresh snow and glacial ice. A transitional state. The Professor does not linger in transitional states.
Isostatic Rebound. The gradual rise of land following the removal of glacial weight. A useful metaphor for the period following a difficult committee meeting.
Nunatak. A peak of rock projecting above a glacier. Isolated, exposed, and entirely unmoved by its surroundings. The Professor considers this admirable.
Terminus. The lowest end of a glacier. The Professor, at sixty, notes that his terminus remains some considerable distance away, and that the gradient is, if anything, increasing.
